## Abstract

Large language models can accelerate engineering work, but their outputs carry no intrinsic epistemic warrant — correct and incorrect claims are generated by the same mechanism and have the same surface properties. This thesis presents Chirality, a formally specified agent operating system that governs LLM-based agents for use in regulated professional engineering practice. Rather than attempting to improve model reliability, the architecture makes the epistemic status of every claim transparent and auditable through four mechanisms: mandatory provenance, no-invention rules, conflict surfacing, and epistemic labeling.

The architecture rests on four philosophical pillars — ontology, epistemology, praxiology, and axiology — with the epistemology identified as the load-bearing pillar whose removal collapses the system's ability to support professional reliance. The epistemology has its own formal ontology: six primitives (claim, warrant, status, gap, conflict, ruling) whose relationships constitute the substrate of the epistemic layer, and a warrant lifecycle (UNWARRANTED → CITED → REVIEWED → AUTHENTICATED) that tracks the epistemic state of claims interleaved with the deliverable production lifecycle. The four pillars are themselves the minimal complete ontology for professional accountability — the necessary and sufficient structure for any context in which a professional takes responsibility for work. Between the chiral components of knowledge — claim and warrant, meaning and commitment, content and accountability — lies a constitutive gap: the space where professional judgment is enacted. The architecture holds this gap open and makes both sides inspectable.

The four-document production kit (Datasheet, Specification, Guidance, Procedure) mirrors the philosophical structure at the deliverable level, a fractal property arising because knowledge at every level has the same chiral structure — complementary, non-superimposable components whose pairing is what knowing means.

The thesis demonstrates that the APEGA practice standard *Relying on the Work of Others and Outsourcing* applies directly to AI agent governance — not by analogy — because the professional's obligations are defined by what the professional must do, not by what the worker is. The Chirality architecture satisfies every requirement of direct supervision and control (APEGA §3.1.1) and thorough review (APEGA §3.1.2) through specific, traceable architectural mechanisms.

The system has been implemented with 38 agent instruction files, 28 deterministic tools, a three-layer invariant system (39 formally stated invariants with four-layer enforcement), and a desktop application. Classical systems engineering disciplines — architecture, configuration management, verification and validation, safety engineering, requirements engineering, control theory, formal methods, and human factors — are not compliance artifacts applied to the agent system but the mechanisms by which agents coordinate, failures are contained, and humans maintain authority.

**Keywords:** systems engineering, LLM agents, professional engineering, epistemic architecture, epistemic ontology, warrant lifecycle, chirality of knowledge, knowledge gap, agent governance, formal invariants, APEGA, professional work products, provenance, write quarantine
