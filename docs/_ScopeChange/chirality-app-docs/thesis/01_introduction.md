# Chapter 1 — Introduction

---

## 1.1 The Problem

Large language models can draft engineering documents, extract dependencies from scope-of-work descriptions, reconcile conflicting specifications, generate cost estimates, and produce analysis reports that read as though a competent engineer wrote them. This capability is transformative. It is also, in its raw form, incompatible with regulated professional engineering practice.

The incompatibility is not that LLMs produce bad output — though they sometimes do. It is that LLM output carries no intrinsic epistemic warrant. A correct claim and an incorrect claim are generated by the same mechanism. They have the same surface properties: fluent prose, specific values, confident tone. The term of art is "hallucination," but the underlying phenomenon is deeper. The model's output is not self-certifying. It cannot be assumed to be grounded in evidence merely because it reads as though it is.

Professional engineering practice is a duty-of-care regime. A licensed professional who authenticates a professional work product — who stamps, seals, and issues it for reliance — is accepting personal, non-delegable responsibility for its accuracy, completeness, and fitness for purpose. This authentication act, under the *Engineering and Geoscience Professions Act* (Alberta) and APEGA practice standards, requires that the professional has either directly supervised and controlled the work or conducted a thorough review of it. Both mechanisms presuppose that the professional can assess the epistemic grounding of the claims in the work — that they can distinguish what is known from what is assumed, what is extracted from what is inferred, and what is supported by evidence from what is not.

An LLM output, absent architectural controls, does not support this assessment. The professional faces a body of text that reads as authoritative but provides no provenance, no epistemic labels, and no indication of where evidence ends and interpolation begins. The thorough review required by regulation becomes, in effect, a complete independent re-derivation — which eliminates the productivity benefit of using AI in the first place.

This thesis addresses the question: **how can systems engineering principles be applied to govern LLM-based agent systems such that their outputs can be relied upon in regulated professional engineering practice?**

---

## 1.2 The Approach

The response presented in this thesis is not to make the model more reliable — though model improvements are welcome and complementary. It is to engineer a governance architecture that makes the epistemic status of every claim transparent and auditable, so that a qualified professional can determine what to rely on.

The architecture, called Chirality, is a formally specified agent operating system for deliverable-heavy professional work. It operates on a foundational decision: all project state lives in git-tracked plain files. There is no external database. The filesystem is the single source of truth. This decision is load-bearing — every capability the system provides (traceability, immutable snapshots, content-addressed approval, the complete audit trail) depends on it.

Agents are organized into three types: Type 0 (Architect) agents define invariant protocols and design standards; Type 1 (Manager) agents orchestrate work through gate-controlled workflows with human authority at every decision junction; Type 2 (Specialist) agents execute bounded tasks within declared write scopes and return auditable outputs. Authority flows downward. Escalation flows upward. No agent of any type can bypass a human gate.

The architecture is governed by three layers of formally stated invariants — workflow design requirements (R1–R9), decomposition invariants (I1–I10), and system-wide invariants (K-*) — enforced through agent instructions, runtime orchestration, human gates, and deterministic tooling.

The system has been implemented as a desktop application with 38 agent instruction files, 28 deterministic tools, and a formal governance document suite. It has been grounded in the APEGA regulatory framework through a professional practice standard that maps the architecture to the specific requirements of the *Relying on the Work of Others and Outsourcing* practice standard.

## 1.3 The Five Contributions

This thesis makes five novel contributions:

**1. An epistemic architecture for LLM reliability transparency.** Four mechanisms — mandatory provenance, no-invention rules, conflict surfacing, and epistemic labeling — make the epistemic status of every claim produced by the system transparent and auditable. The system does not try to prevent hallucination; it makes unsupported claims structurally visible. This responds to a gap identified in the literature: existing approaches focus on reducing hallucination rates (a population-level improvement), while professional practice requires per-claim epistemic transparency.

**2. A regulatory mapping grounding AI agent governance in existing professional obligations.** The thesis demonstrates that APEGA's *Relying on the Work of Others and Outsourcing* standard applies directly to AI agent governance — not by analogy — because the professional's obligations are defined by what the professional must do, not by what the worker is. This shows that AI agent governance can be achieved within existing regulatory frameworks, without requiring new AI-specific regulation.

**3. A four-pillar philosophical framework with fractal instantiation.** The architecture rests on ontology (what exists), epistemology (what can be known), praxiology (how work is done), and axiology (what the system values). The epistemology is identified as the load-bearing pillar, and it has its own formal ontology: six primitives (claim, warrant, status, gap, conflict, ruling) and a warrant lifecycle (UNWARRANTED → CITED → REVIEWED → AUTHENTICATED) that tracks the epistemic state of claims interleaved with the deliverable lifecycle. The four pillars are themselves the minimal complete ontology for professional accountability. The four-document production kit (Datasheet, Specification, Guidance, Procedure) mirrors the philosophical structure — a fractal property arising from the fact that accountability requires the same four things at every level of abstraction.

**4. A fault containment architecture through write scope quarantine.** The Type 0/1/2 agent hierarchy combined with declared write scopes creates formal fault containment zones. A Type 2 agent failure cannot corrupt source truth. This adapts classical SE fault containment to the governance of probabilistic agents.

**5. A three-layer invariant system with four-layer enforcement.** Thirty-nine formally stated invariants are enforced through agent instructions, runtime orchestration, human gates, and deterministic tooling. This constitutes a formal methods contribution applied to a novel domain: the governance of LLM-based agent systems.

## 1.4 Thesis Structure

The thesis is organized as follows:

- **Chapter 2 (Literature Review)** surveys five domains — AI agent architectures, LLM reliability, formal methods and safety-critical SE, professional regulation, and epistemic frameworks — and identifies the gap that motivates this work.
- **Chapter 3 (Philosophical Framework)** presents the four pillars, argues that the epistemology is load-bearing, and demonstrates the fractal property.
- **Chapter 4 (Architecture)** presents the technical architecture: filesystem-as-state, entity model, lifecycle state machine, type hierarchy, write scope, invariant system, orchestration, and contracts.
- **Chapter 5 (Epistemic Architecture)** develops the core novelty claim: the six epistemic primitives, the four enforcement mechanisms, the warrant lifecycle as operational model, worked examples, and comparison to alternative approaches.
- **Chapter 6 (Professional Practice Integration)** presents the APEGA regulatory mapping and the argument that AI agents are "others" under §3.0.
- **Chapter 7 (SE Design Analysis)** examines the architecture through eight SE disciplines.
- **Chapter 8 (Implementation and Validation)** presents the working implementation: 38 agents, 28 tools, desktop application, and validation mechanisms.
- **Chapter 9 (Discussion)** assesses limitations, generalizability, and future work.
- **Chapter 10 (Conclusion)** restates the contributions and answers the research question.

Appendix A provides the full invariant catalog. Appendix B provides the complete agent inventory. Appendix C provides the detailed APEGA regulatory mapping.

## 1.5 Scope and Limitations

This thesis presents the architecture, its philosophical foundation, its regulatory grounding, and its implementation. It does not present:

- Empirical validation through controlled studies with practicing engineers (identified as the highest-priority future work in Chapter 9)
- A regulatory ruling or endorsement by APEGA (the regulatory mapping is the firm's interpretation, not a regulator's pronouncement)
- A claim that the architecture eliminates LLM hallucination (it makes hallucination visible, not absent)
- A claim that instruction-level enforcement is sufficient alone (the architecture relies on multi-layer defense in depth)

The contribution is architectural, not empirical. The thesis demonstrates that a governance architecture for AI agents in professional practice can be designed, specified, implemented, and grounded in existing regulation. Whether it improves professional review in measured practice is a question for the empirical study that this architecture makes possible.
